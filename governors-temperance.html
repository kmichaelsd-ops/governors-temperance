Governors: Temperance

The Question

Barfort, Klemmensen, and Larsen (2021) collected the lifespans of 3,587 candidates for US gubernatorial office going back to the 1800s, in order to estimate the causal effect of winning office on longevity. By comparing winners and losers of close elections — near coin flips — they argue the treatment is as good as randomly assigned. (See Gelman’s commentary on the paper, and the authors’ response, for a running argument about whether the headline estimate is believable.)

There are two scenarios. Focus on just one of them:

  • Imagine you work for a life insurer, and want to forecast how long candidates for the US Senate might live based on their age, party, election result, and other variables.

  • Imagine you are a researcher. You want to know if winning candidates live longer. You are interested in candidates for any elected office — senate, governor, mayor, et cetera.

The life insurer asks: How many years, on average, will this year’s US Senate candidates live after their election, given their age, party, and election result?

The researcher asks: What is the average causal effect of winning an election — for any elected office, from senator to governor to mayor — rather than losing it, on the number of years a candidate lives afterward?

Note that we use the same data, and the same fitted model, to help both people.

The setup chunk of this document carries forward the work from Wisdom and Courage: the close-election tibble x and the fitted model fit_governors are already built, so today starts at the answering.

Why is the estimate from avg_comparisons() so much larger than the difference between the two group averages from avg_predictions()?

The difference between the group average predictions (2.4 years) is calculated using the observed values of the covariates in our sample, where winners and losers have different covariate distributions. Most importantly, winners have positive win margins (0 to 5) and losers have negative win margins (-5 to 0). Since win_margin has a negative coefficient of -1.46, the positive win margins of winners lower their predicted lifespans, while the negative win margins of losers increase their predicted lifespans, pulling the two group averages closer together.

In contrast, avg_comparisons() computes the average causal effect (treatment effect) by comparing the predicted lifespans under the ‘Win’ vs. ‘Lose’ conditions for each candidate while holding all other covariates (including win_margin) constant. This isolates the true treatment coefficient of winning, which is 8.63 years.

Interpretation

How does the life insurer interpret the estimated effect of winning? How does the researcher interpret the same number differently?

  • Life Insurer (Predictive Interpretation): The insurer views the 8.6-year estimate purely as a predictive adjustment. Knowing that a Senate candidate won the election is a useful signal for forecasting their longevity. It does not matter why winners live longer, only that winning is a strong predictor to help price policies and manage liabilities.
  • Researcher (Causal Interpretation): The researcher interprets the 8.6-year estimate as the average causal effect of winning office. Because close elections mimic a randomized experiment (a regression discontinuity design), they assume winning causes candidates to live longer, likely due to increased wealth, access to better healthcare, higher social status, or reduced stress.

Humility

Besides the average years lived, identify two additional quantities of interest the person in your scenario might care about.

  • Life Insurer:
    1. The probability of surviving past a certain age threshold (e.g., 75 or 80 years) to price annuities.
    2. The standard deviation or variance of post-election lifespans to assess risk and tail longevity probabilities.
  • Researcher:
    1. The heterogeneous treatment effects (e.g., does winning benefit younger candidates more than older ones, or Democrats more than Republicans?).
    2. The effect of winning on health-adjusted life years (quality of life) rather than just raw lifespan.

Why might these estimates be a poor answer to your scenario’s question? Identify at least two possible sources of bias.

  • Life Insurer:
    1. Representativeness Bias: The model is estimated on gubernatorial candidates from 1945–2012, which may not generalize to this year’s Senate candidates due to differences between offices and modern medical advancements.
    2. Selection Bias: The model is fit only on close elections (\(\le 5\%\) margin). If the relationship between winning and longevity is different in landslide elections, the model will produce poor forecasts for candidates running in non-close races.
  • Researcher:
    1. Generalizability Bias (External Validity): The causal effect for governors may not generalize to other elected offices like mayors or school board members, where the stress, resources, and prestige differ.
    2. Confounding Bias: Even in close elections, winning might not be perfectly random. If candidates who win close elections differ systematically from those who lose (e.g., having more physical resilience or better party support), unobserved factors could bias the causal estimate.
